River safari in croc-infested waters!


Kunal   Posted on 26th December, 2016

The legendary Chambal River, feared by many for decades as the region was notoriously known for dacoits (bandits), gang wars, sand mining mafia, feudal and caste rivalries and dirty politics. Unfortunately, not many know or appreciate Chambal as one of the best river based eco-systems in India, supporting several species of wildlife both endemic and migratory to the region. Some of the species include the ‘gharial’ or the fish-eating crocodile, ‘mugger’ or the Indian marsh crocodile, seven species of fresh-water turtles, several species of birds including the skimmer and also the extremely rare South Asian river dolphin.

A juvenile ‘Mugger’ Crocodile at the National Chambal Sanctuary

Thanks to the fear caused by bandits of the Chambal Valley, the river was un-touched by pollution and human abuse for several decades. If you’re keen to learn more about the bandits of the Chambal see the movie – The Bandit Queen.

Bar-headed Geese flying over the Chambal River

In the late 20th century the government made a concerted effort and several gangs were brought to justice and some surrendered as well. With the bandit menace over, tourism and other industries arrived in hordes and one such hidden gem and a must see for all nature and wildlife enthusiasts is the Chambal River Safari in the National Chambal Sanctuary. The river safari is an eco-tourism initiative launched by the tourism departments of Madhya Pradesh and Rajasthan.

Chambal River Safari Landmark

I believe there are a few areas along the Chambal River where you can do the river safari. I visited the one less than an hour from Agra and four kilometers after the town of Dholpur in Madhya Pradesh. You will know you’re there when you approach a bridge and along the river’s bank you will see several cars and boats. Depending on the weather conditions, the first boat leaves at 8:00 hours and the last trip is around 16:30 hours. The best time to do the safari is in the winter – November to early March. I wouldn’t recommend going for the safari on days when there is heavy fog – you won’t see much and the drive to the safari may not be very safe.

Massive dunes overlooking the Chambal River

My first safari was for an hour (three kilometer up-stream and back). This cost us about INR 500 per person and maximum of eight adults (including the boatman and a guide) can be in the boat at a time. The entire boat costs INR 2,400 (US$35) for an hour and if you have a camera, that’s another INR 50. If you really like nature and are into wildlife photography then I would recommend doing the three-hour safari which goes on for 18 Km on the river (one way).

Getting ready to board our boat for the Chambal River Safari

After filling in some minor paperwork, we were given life jackets and soon got into one of these boats. Carry lots of water, a hat and put on sunscreen if your skin is sensitive. If you’re going for the three-hour safari you can bring some food as well. The boats didn’t look very sturdy at first and I thought twice about what I was getting myself in to – cruising in this boat with crocodiles lurking below the brackish water! Man, I must be out of my mind!

Indian marsh crocodile sun-bathing

However, our guide assured us that there have been no incidents of crocodiles attacking the boats since the safari program was launched and without wasting a second, we set sail.

Cruising on the Chambal River

We hadn’t even gone much further when we saw our first adult ‘mugger’ crocodile – hanging tight and right next to the where we on-boarded the boat! This river safari is no joke!

Mouth wide open, Indian River Crocodile, Chambal River

The crocodiles were used to seeing humans on boats and did not make a dash for the water as we cruised down the river.

Flock of Bar-headed Geese by the Chambal River

We also saw several species of migratory birds like the Ruddy Shelduck or Brahminy duck which is a resident of Central Asia, bar-headed geese, also from Central Asia, grey heron, stone plover, river lapwing and others.

Bonelli’s Eagle – National Chambal Sanctuary

Amongst the raptors in this region, we spotted the Osprey and the Bonelli’s Eagle.

Indian Skimmers – an endangered species of the Chambal River

On my second visit to the National Chambal Sanctuary, we were lucky to spot some 20 Indian Skimmers. This time I accompanied the extremely knowledgeable - Delhi Bird Photographers, a Facebook group of hardcore birders from various walks of life and we did the three-hour safari as recommended by the local guides.

Softshell Turtles also called Pancake Turtles, National Chambal Sanctuary

Out of the seven species of fresh water turtles that reside in the Chambal we saw two – the bigger ones are the Trionychidae or softshells or pancake turtle and the smaller - Indian tent turtle

Development over the Chambal River

On the way back I couldn’t but imagine what this place would have been in the 1960s / 1970s. My father used to go to Gwalior for work when he was in his teens and when boats were used as the major mode of transport to ferry people, automobiles, bullock carts etc. across the river.

Mugger and Gharial face-off, Chambal River Safari

He also mentioned that no one dared step into the murky waters of the Chambal river back then unlike these brave folks on rubber tyres and tubes who risk their lives everyday to collect lose change thrown by people in to the river.

Locals using magnets to collect coins thrown in the Chambal River

Today, bridges have been constructed for automobiles and trains and several villages have popped up along the banks of the Chambal.

Agricultural development along the Chambal River

The river’s water is used for agricultural and personal purposes while some use the river for it’s rich sand deposits which is transported often illegally from the river banks by sand mining mafia.

Sand mining in the Chambal River

Sand mining is only authorized during the monsoon season when the sand deposited is abundant. However, I saw several people digging sand and loading it in tractors.

Co-existence of man and Gharial at the National Chambal Sanctuary

Seeing this co-existence of man with animal may surprise and may make many angry and although from a distance it may seem like everything is working perfectly fine but in reality – it is us, humans who are winning this race!

Bocas Del Toro - ecotourism & adventure in Panama

Evelyne Meyer   Posted on 18 June, 2015

When you think of Panama, the first thing that will come to your mind is the famous Panama Canal, a financial haven offering investment opportunities, or a retirement destination for “gringos”. But one should not overlook an even more important aspect of the country’s potential, as one of the most popular ecotourism destinations, thanks to its abundance of fauna and wildlife. Panama truly has it all: mountains, cloud forest, oceans and jungle, and even though the list of places to visit is very long, I would like to focus more specifically on the archipelago of Bocas del Toro, located in the country's northwestern Caribbean sea. If you like hiking, you can start your adventure in Old Bank on Isla Bastimentos, and stop for a coffee at the famous organic farm called “Up in the Hill” as well as beaches such as Long Beach, Playa Pelicano or Sendero Caiman to your to-do list.

Bocas del Toro, also known as the Galapagos of the Caribbean, separates Almirante Bay and Laguna de Chiriquí from the open Caribbean Sea. Besides earning its fame from the era of the United Fruit Company, Bocas del Toro is starting to be known for its biodiversity. The Smithsonian Tropical Research Institute of Panama has setup a station on Isla Colon, conducting research as well as engaging in conservation efforts of the endangered endemic species. The waters surrounding the islands of the archipelago are home to more than 95% of the coral species found in the caribbean sea, and over 32 mammal species have been recorded on the islands.

By definition, “ecotourism” means travelling responsibly without disrupting the ecosystem By definition, “ecotourism” means travelling responsibly without disrupting the ecosystem and many other activities that are nature friendlyS, while improving the wellbeing of the local community in a sustainable manner. Many will agree that the Bastimentos National Marine Park is the number one location when it comes to ecotourism in Bocas del Toro, offering exciting adventures such as surfing, sailing, hiking, wildlife observation and many other activities that are nature friendly.​

If you like hiking, you can start your adventure in Old Bank on Isla Bastimentos, and stop for a coffee at the famous organic farm called “Up the Hill”. From there you can keep walking towards Wizard Beach, or even Red Frog Beach, if you are up for the challenge. This is just one of the many paths the Bastimentos National Park has to offer, and you should also add a hike through the jungle in Salt Creek, a visit to the bat cave, as well as beaches such as Long Beach, Playa Pelicano or Sendero Caiman to your to-do list.

Water sports amateurs and surfers will particularly enjoy the northern shore of Bastimentos, which offers some of the best surf breaks and waves, as well as endless strips of empty white sand beaches including Playa Primera, Wizard Beach, Playa Segunda, Red Frog Beach, Polo Beach and Long Beach or Playa Larga.


Wildlife and animal lovers can’t miss out on watching the various species of as well as endangered species such as the famous red poison-dart frog as well as endangered species such as the famous red poison-dart frog (Oophaga pumilio) sea turtles during the nesting or hatching seasons on this island. Please remember to leave your camera at home, as the bright light of your flash will disorient and disturb the turtles. You will also be able to spot exotic animals such as manatees, bottlenose dolphins, sloths, howler monkeys, rays, toucans, snakes, caimans.

If snorkeling is what you are looking for, take a short boat trip to Cayo Coral (Crawl Cay) at the southern tip, which is separated by a shallow channel from Isla Popa. The transparent waters on the southeast side of the island offer ideal conditions to explore marine life, including abundant and colorful coral reefs, nurse sharks, as well as a large number of tropical fishes and other sea creatures.

From here, you are only a short boat ride away from Cayos Zapatilla (Zapatilla Cays), a group of two uninhabited islands located east of Isla Bastimentos. These two paradisiac islands lie within the limits of the Bastimentos National Marine Park. The northern island is 14 hectares long while south Cayo Zapatilla is more than double its size

Are you an avid birder? You will find your nirvana in Bocas del Toro’s jungles and coasts, where you can observe many different species, including Toucans, Oropendolas and different types of parrots. Bird Island was named that way - as you may have guessed - because it is a true bird watching paradise. Here you will find the only known nesting place of the Red-billed Tropic Bird (Phaeton aethereus) in this region.

Last but not least, you may want to learn more about the indigenous communities and their local produce. The Ngobe Bugle are the largest indigenous group present in the Bocas del Toro Archipelago, and are scattered in tight communities all over Isla Colon, Isla Bastimentos, Isla Solarte, Isla Cristobal, Isla Popa and Water Cay. In order to get to know their lifestyle and witness their work at first hand, I would highly recommend you to join the Oreba Chocolate Tour and learn how organic chocolate is produced. Oreba means chocolate in the Ngobe language, and when you purchase their organic chocolate, you are actively supporting the indigenous farmers and their families, as well as Fair Trade in Panama. Today, more than 30 indigenous families are part of the Oreba Cooperative, and the proceeds of the tours are re-distributed to the farmers themselves, and to help fund local schools and other business projects.

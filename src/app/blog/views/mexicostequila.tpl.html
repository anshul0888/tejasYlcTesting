Mexico’s Tequila Trail - The Best Way To See Tequila Country


Tour of Tequila Distillery

Fern   Posted on 23 October, 2016

For all you tequila fanatics, you finally have the chance to not only visit the origin of this golden drink, but to also sample some of the finest tequila the region has to offer. The tequila trail winds its way through the picturesque countryside of Jalisco, Mexico, passing countless haciendas whose sole purpose is cultivating blue agave to produce the highest quality tequila in the world.

Tequila is the traditional spirit of Mexico, made from the blue agave plant. Even to this day all 100% agave tequila is produced in Tequila Country in Mexico. There are five different types of tequila, which are all classified depending on the amount of time they have been left to age. Blanco tequila and Oro tequila are the youngest, only left to settle for up to 2 months, Reposado is little older, left to age for up to 2 years in wooden barrels, Añejo is a very well aged tequila, allowed to age in the wooden barrels for up to 3 years. Anything over 3 years is classed as Extra Añejo, and this tequila is so strong it must be diluted with distilled water. As tequila get’s older, or more mature, the flavour deepens, becomes smoother and more complex. This is why the Añejo and Extra Añejo tequilas are the most expensive.

Tequila Country was named a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2006, due to its historically rich te-quila producing towns and unique agave landscapes. Although tequila is fast becoming one of the most produced spirits throughout the world, in Mexico it is classed as an extremely traditional, al-most ritual-like process. Jalisco alone is home to over 700 brands of tequila, many of which you can visit during the tequila trail.

Tequila Trail and Express Train (Maplets)

You can embark on your very own tequila trail by renting a car in Guadalajara, or you can opt for a more traditional mode of transport, the Tequila Express train. Organised tequila trails tend to last 12 hours, but if you wish to plan your own, you could even make a weekend out of it. Whichever mode of transport you choose, there will be plenty for you to see and sample. As you work your way down to the town of tequila, you will pass many distilleries, both extremely big brands and small boutique labels. However, no matter what your agenda for the tequila trail, you should not miss the four most popular distilleries in Jalisco; Jose Cuervo, Casa Herradura, Sauza and La Cofradia. All the distilleries mentioned here are child friendly, however, it is wise to call ahead to the smaller distilleries and check their policies.

Jose Cuervo

From the Mundo Cuervo visitor centre you can request samples and tours from every step of the glorious production process. Why not visit Jose Cuervo’s oldest distillery plant, La Rojeña. Here you can see the old wooden barrels used to age the tequila, ask questions about the distilling process, or simply enjoy the smell of the rich tequila. Upon request you can also take a sneak peek inside the Reserva de la Familia cellar. Reserve de la Familia is Jose Cuervo’s most precious and exclusive tequila, aged for over 3 years in this very hacienda.

Tequila Trail: Jose Cuervo in a Barrell

If you are specifically interested in this distillery, then you can book a trip on the Jose Cuervo Ex-press. This luxurious train will pick you up from Guadalajara Station, take you all the way to the beautiful town of Tequila, and make a stop of the Jose Cuervo distillery. The train is 100% child friendly, and children under 5 ride for free.

Tequila Trail Express Bar

Casa Herradura

Out of the 156 distillers in Tequila Country, Casa Herradura is the only original factory, in its original location. Built in 1870, this world-class hacienda is steeped in a rich history. You can really feel the pride in the workers at Casa Herradura, as they explain to you the complex process of tequila production. Included in the tour of this magnificent distillery is several tastings highlighting each stage of the ageing process. Walk among the 20 foot high stacks of oak barrels, housing the pre-cious tequila as it undergoes the final stages of the production process.

Learning the ancient and modern tequila making process (Casa Herradura)

Another unique aspect of the Casa Herradura tour is that you can hop on a horse, and ride out into the agave fields. Here you can watch the skilled workers dig out the huge agave plants, or you can even pick up your own coa (long shovel-like tool) and try out your agave hacking skills.

Casa Sauza

One of the oldest distilleries in Jalisco, Casa Sauza is home to one of the most popular Mexican tequila on the market today, Hornitos.

Most popular tequila: Hornitos Sauza (Casa Sauza)

As soon as you step into this beautiful hacienda, you are met by a traditional Jimador (a farmer that harvests the blue agave plant). He will then take you out to the agave field where he will teach you how to dig up the agave plant, so be prepared to get down and dirty. You will then proceed to the next leg of the tour, where you will see the entire Casa Sauza tequila making process, from milling and fermenting to the distillation, ageing and finally, bottling of the tequila.

Tequila Train Blue Agave Plant (Borongaja)

After your fascinating tour, the good people of Casa Sauza will take you out into the gardens of the hacienda, where you can sample their delicious tequila whilst being serenaded by the sound of the surrounding fountains. Here you can also try their brand new gourmet menu, serving Mexican fu-sion dishes, made to pair with their lovely Hornitos tequila.

Mexican Fusion Food (Casa Sauza)

La Cofradia

At this hacienda, they believe tequila is a very sensual spirit. This is why the owners of La Cofradia created the ‘Tour of The Senses’, an evening tour that takes guests through the sights, smells, feel and sounds of the tequila making process. Also available at this state of the art distillery are hands-on tours, where you can make your own tequila, help out in the La Cofradia tequila making pro-cesses, and even design your own bottle.

If you are organising your own tequila trail, then you may want to consider making La Cofradia your last stop. This stunning hacienda also has a lovely boutique hotel, so you can relax and take a load off after a long day of tequila sampling.

Four days in Cuba


Kunal   Posted on 16th December, 2016

If you’re traveling to Cuba for less than a week and if this is your first time there, then you should definitely explore Havana and plan on staying for at least 3 nights.

Habana 1791: Perfume shop in Old Havana

A big bustling and fast developing city, you will see traces of its old world charm as you will see several gas guzzling American vintage cars as taxis.

I did not stay in Old Havana but in Vedado, a residential neighborhood and a 4-5 CUC taxi ride to Old Havana. Old Havana is where all the tourists end up and is also close to most of the sightseeing, restaurants, cafes etc. If you don’t mind the crowds and chaos then you should stay in Old Havana.

Limestone mountains in Vinales Valley, Cuba

If you feel you’ve had enough of Havana after 3 nights you can plan a day trip to either Vinales, which is in the western part of the Island and the hub of tobacco plantations or the super touristy beach town of Veradado, similar to Cancun in Mexico – fancy and modern all-inclusive resorts with great beaches. I chose to do a day trip to Vinales and below you’ll find out exactly why!

Tour of Havana on a hop-on hop-off bus

On my first day in Havana I decided to get a feel of the city’s layout and took a hop-on hop-off bus from Parque Central in Centro. The tour took about 2 hours and stopped at all major points of interest in the city. Once the tour was over I had a much better idea of which areas I wanted to delve deeper into and which areas to avoid given that I was in Cuba for a very limited period of time. Here is my list of the best that Havana has to offer:

Top attractions in Centro Havana:

The majestic Gran Teatro de La Habana

Gran Teatro de La Habana is the Cuban capital’s answer to Boston Symphony Hall and home to the Cuban National Ballet and a variety of arts, festivals and performances are also organized here. This beautiful piece of architecture is even grander at night when it is all lit up.

El Capitolio is a replica of the US Capitol Hill

Capitolio Nacional or El Capitolio was the seat of the Cuban government until the Cuban revolution in 1959. This monument was designed to look like the US Capitol Hill, which it no doubt does. Too bad when I visited it was closed as there was some on-going restoration work.

Elegant hotel lobby in Havana

There are several historic hotels in this part of town such as Hotel Telegrafo, Inglaterra, Parque Central. Just walk in and you’ll be amazed with the décor in the hotels’ lobbies. I saw a few outdoor cafes and restaurants in the ground floor of the hotels and was tempted to sit and eat there but I took Ana’s advice and avoided these touristy, expensive joints with really bad service!

Where to eat in Centro Havana:

Pork barbequed in Cuban style on a skewer

La Terraza is at the corner of Hotel Parque Central on Paseo de Marti street. This building has three floors each with a restaurant serving a different cuisine. First floor is Italian, second is traditional Cuban and the third is the terrace specializing just in Cuban barbeque. Without wasting a second, I opted for the terrace! I ordered a juicy skewer of pork marinated in garlic and lemon and had a couple of Bucaneros to go along with the food. Great food but just a bit pricey!

Top attractions in the Plaza de la Revolucion area:

Artwork on the late Fidel Casto next to the Plaza de la Revolucion

Plaza de la Revolucion or the Revolution Square is where all the political rallies take place and is the backdrop in several images and videos you may have seen of Fidel Castro giving powerful speeches to the Cuban people.

Malecon drive from Old Havana to Vedado

Malecon: This 8km long roadway lets you cruise from one end of Havana to the other. This is a popular late evening hang out spot for the locals and you will often see people playing music, dancing or fishing. One of the best ways to enjoy a drive along this coastal road is to hire a classic American open-top car which you can rent starting at 25 CUCs per hour.

Drive on the Malecon in an American Vintage Car

If renting an Oldsmobile is out of your budget, you can grab a couple of Bucaneros or Cristals (Cuban beers) and watch the sunset, attempt to strike a conversation with the locals, shoot some amazing photographs and perhaps even get to see a massive cruise liner pass by. To get to Malecon, if you’re walking from Centro just keep going north towards the coast on Paseo de Marti.

Top attractions in Old Havana or La Habana Vieja

Old Havana starts east of Parque Central. The streets leading to Old Havana are mostly car free and walking is the preferred way to explore the oldest part of the city.

Plaza de la Cathedral, Old Havana

Plaza del la Cathedral is a beautiful square with some magnificent mansions and a baroque style cathedral dating back to the 18th century. It is also home to several restaurants and a couple of art galleries. I overheard a tour guide who said this area used to be a swamp back in the day!

Plaza Vieja, Old Havana

Plaza Vieja or the Old Square also has several colonial buildings now converted to restaurants and also has a couple of hotels

Street Carnival in Old Havana

Historically this was the site of several executions, bull fighting, carnivals and processions and I’m not in the least bit surprised why it wouldn’t be.

The view from Castilo de la Real Fuerza

Castilo de la Real Feurza or the Castle of the Royal Forces was originally designed to fight off pirates off the coast of Havana during the 16th century. Over time it ended up as a library and today it stands as a maritime museum. The entry is 3 CUC, unfortunately there was no English speaking guide and the description of the artifacts in the museum were all in Spanish.

View of Havana Bay and Morro Castle

However, the views from the watchtower were outstanding and well worth the entrance fee!

Plaza de Armas at night

Plaza de Armas or the Weapons Square is the oldest square in the city spanning four centuries. It was built to showcase military parades and musical concerts and today is home to several restaurants, Hotel Santa Isabel (a beautiful historic hotel) and several open-air bookstalls and flea market. You can get to this square by walking through Calle (street) Obispo from Parque Central.

The bustling Obispo Street in Old Havana

Obispo Street has dozens of convenience stores, restaurants, bars and art galleries worth visiting. If you’re short of CUCs there are some banks here to exchange your money.

Hotel Ambos Mundos (pink) where Ernest Hemingway stayed in 1930s

Obispo Street is also home to Hotel Ambos Mundos (the Wi-Fi is best outside this hotel), a beautiful early 20th century hotel, which was home to the famous American writer Ernest Hemingway in the 1930s.

Plaza de San Francisco at night

Plaza de San Francisco is close to where the cruise ships dock in Havana. It also has a church by the same name – San Francisco church which also has a monastery. Historically this square was used to organize fairs and later on gambling activities as well. Today, the square has restaurants, cafes and a Benetton clothing store!

Musio de la Revolucion in Havana

Musio de la Revolucion or the Museum of the Revolution sits in what used to the Presidential Palace but was converted to a museum after the Cuban revolution. This museum is the only one out of several in Havana that I visited for it depicted three floors of Cuban history from pre-colonial and Cuban fight for independence from Spain and the revolutionary war of the 1950/60s. Absolutely recommend it if you like history and have read or see movies on Fidel Castro and Che Guevara.

Where to eat and drink in Old Havana:

Grilled prawns

Sidewalk restaurant in Plaza de Armas: Decent out-door restaurant with a live band and great seafood but again a bit pricey.

Ice-cream sandwich from Musio del Chocalate

Musio del Chocolate is not really a museum but more of a shop selling chocolates and a walk-in café. I had an ice-cream sandwich and a cold chocolate shake, both top grade, highly recommended and less than 1 CUC each! Not bad at all just when I was just about to give up on finding reasonably priced food in Havana!

Café Paris on Obispo Street for a quick bite and a couple of mojitos was just what I needed after a long day of walking in Old Havana.

Los Frailes – a religious themed hotel with staff dressed as monks

Hotel Los Frailes: Definitely stop by this religious-themed hotel to go back in time when you see the hotel staff dress as monks and its Spanish colonial gorgeous interior. You can get a coffee or a mohito from the bar.

What to buy in Cuba:

Hand rolled Cuban Cigars

Cuban Cigars: No trip to Cuba is complete without buying at least one Cuban cigar. Now you can purchase quite a few Cuban cigars at very decent prices without any documentation. I was recommended to buy authentic Cuban cigars from either a shop inside a good hotel or better still from a government authorized shop. I purchased my stash of Cohiba and Montecristo brand Cuban cigars from La Casa del Habano in Od Havana.

The best Cuban rum: Santiago de Cuba

Cuban Rum: More my poison than the Cuban cigars, Ana recommended I pick up a couple of bottles of the locals favorite – Santiago de Cuba rum aged 7+ years which is exactly what I did. After wrapping them tight in my check-in baggage I bought two bottles of Havana Club at the duty free just because I needed to get rid of the left over CUCs that I had.

Day trip to Vinales Valley:

Beautiful Vinales Valley, Cuba

Absolutely unplanned, no clue and zero expectations but a desire to see more of Cuba than just Havana, made me do a day trip to Vinales Valley in the Pinar del Rio province of Cuba. Vinales Valley is 2.5 hours and 180km from the capital. I booked a day tour from a travel desk at one of the hotels in Centro Havana. The tour was 70 CUCs and included transportation in an air-conditioned coach, visit to a cigar factory and a tobacco farm, visit to the Mural de la Prehistoria, boat ride through an underground Indian cave and lunch which by the way was very average as is the case in most organized tours.

Lush green mountains and tobacco plantations in Vinales Valley

It is when you drive to Vinales Valley through the lush green countryside that you’re exposed to the kind of Cuban life one that literally takes you back in time. People commuting in horse carriages and carts, farmers using traditional agricultural techniques, shepherds walking their herd along the highway, sugarcane and tobacco plantations and just downright simplistic rural living – something you don’t see everywhere anymore! Not to forget the awesome views of the mountains and limestone cliffs surrounding the valley

Included in the tour was a visit to a cigar factory. No cameras were allowed inside. Several workers were rolling cigars by hand, the more you roll the more you get paid!

Tobacco farm in Vinales Valley, Cuba

We also visited a tobacco farm where the local farmer had just planted tobacco seeds which would be ready for harvest by March the following year.

How to roll a Cuban Cigar

He showed us the fine art of picking the best tobacco leaves, cutting them and rolling them into cigars and cigar aficionados had an opportunity to buy some home made cigars from him as well.

After lunch we proceeded to an underground Indian cave, which had a river flowing through it. These underground cave rivers are the only source of fresh water in Cuba.

Mural de La Prehistoria painted by a Cuban artist in the 1960s

Our last stop was Mural de la Prehistoria, a 120 meter long painting made on a cliff by the artist - Leovigildo González Morillo in the 1960s. It took him and his team four years to complete this piece of art! We also had fresh pina coladas at the bar on site, which was absolutely refreshing. Unlike other places, the bartender preferred we help ourselves to the rum in our. He gave us the bottle of rum and we were free to pour in as much as we wanted!
